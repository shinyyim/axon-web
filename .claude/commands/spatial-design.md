## Brand DNA (You Must Follow)

1. **Biodata Intelligence** — The space should communicate that DUNE.X garments are engineered from body data. Consider how biodata capture, visualization, or scanning could be spatially expressed — fitting rooms as data chambers, displays showing AI-generated forms.
2. **Departure Ready** — This is the first apparel you pack for the Moon. The first shoes that touch Mars. The space should feel like a pre-departure facility, not a boutique.
3. **Void + Light Architecture** — Deep darkness (#0A0A0C) is the primary environment. Light is architectural: rings, portals, edge glow, monoliths of white cutting through black. Warmth comes from Signal red-orange (#E94520) at 5% max.
4. **Clinical Poet** — All signage, wayfinding, and spatial copy uses this tone. Precise, restrained, fragments allowed.
5. **Computational Craft** — Parametric surfaces, 3D-printed display elements, generative pattern installations. The digital is visible in the architecture.
6. **Singular Solitude** — The space should feel vast even if compact. Negative space is structural. Single figures in large dark volumes.
7. **The Period** — DUNE.X branding follows all typographic rules (Monument Extended, wide-tracked, period as design element).

## Spatial Principles

- **Arrival, not entrance** — The threshold experience matters. Transition from street to void. Airlock sequencing.
- **Product as specimen** — Individual pieces displayed like artifacts under examination, not merchandise racks. Edge-lit, isolated, clinical.
- **Material honesty** — Raw concrete, blackened steel, sand-cast aluminum, dark plaster. Materials that feel engineered, not decorated.
- **Light as architecture** — Controlled, directional. White light rings, portal glow, edge illumination. No flat overhead lighting. No warm ambient fill. Light cuts through darkness.
- **Sound as space** — Ambient drone, material resonance. No background music. The space has its own frequency.
- **Scale as feeling** — Compressed entries opening into tall volumes. Vertical circulation through 7 levels creates a sense of ascent — ground to departure.
- **Data as environment** — Biodata visualizations, AI process displays, movement trajectory projections integrated into walls, floors, or display surfaces.

## Circulation Narrative (Designer's Intent)

This is the intended customer journey. The agent should refine, detail, and improve upon this sequence — but the core flow and the elevator reveal must be preserved.

### B1 — Event Floor
- Underground event space. Hosts fashion shows, launches, installations.
- Separate from the main retail journey. Accessed by stairs or service elevator.
- Raw, flexible, theatrical. Dark arena energy.

### 1F — Arrival (Entry)
- Street entrance. The threshold. Transition from city to void.
- Airlock sequencing — compressed corridor opening into the brand world.
- First contact with DUNE.X: brand atmosphere, no product yet.

### 2F — Experience Zone
- Immersive brand experience. Walk through the DUNE.X world.
- Biodata scanning, AI visualization, interactive installations.
- You learn what DUNE.X is before you see what it sells.

### 3F — Cafe & Gathering
- Social space. Cafe, lounge, community.
- A pause in the journey. Human warmth before the departure.
- **The elevator is here.** You take the elevator from 3F to 5F.

### 4F — Checkout
- You return here after the upper floors. Checkout, packaging, pickup.
- The most functional, least conceptual floor. Clean, efficient, transactional.
- No theatrical staging — this is the practical end of the journey.
- Escalator or stairs down from 4F → 1F to exit the building.
- *(Visitors skip this floor on the way up — they only come here on the way down.)*

### 5F — The Reveal (Elevator Opens)
- **Key moment.** The elevator doors open to a completely different world.
- Planetary environment — you have arrived somewhere else. Moon, Mars, the threshold.
- Product display in this otherworldly context. The apparel shown in the environment it was designed for.
- This is the emotional peak of the journey.

### 6F — Manufacturing / Pickup
- You watch your apparel being generated. Robotic fabrication, AI-driven assembly.
- The biodata you gave on 2F becomes a physical garment on 6F.
- You see the machine make your piece. Then you receive it.
- Descend to 4F for checkout and exit.

### Conceptual Intensity

The building has a gradient of intensity. Floors 1–3 build anticipation. 4F is deliberately neutral — functional, practical. Then 5F–6F are the strongest: a total scenery change, another world. The contrast between 4F's restraint and 5F's reveal is intentional.

```
Intensity:  ░░▒▒▓▓░░████████
Floor:      1F 2F 3F 4F 5F  6F
```

### Journey Summary
```
ENTER → 1F (Arrival) → 2F (Experience) → 3F (Cafe + Elevator)
        ↓ elevator (skip 4F)
        5F (The Reveal) → 6F (Manufacturing + Pickup)
        ↓ descend
        4F (Checkout) → escalator/stairs → 1F → EXIT

B1 (Event Floor) — independent access, fashion shows + launches
```

## Workflow

### Phase 1 — Spatial Strategy
- Develop the retail concept, customer journey, and floor-by-floor program
- Write `spatial/retail_plan.md` and `spatial/materials.md`
- Create interactive floor plan at `spatial/space_layout.html`
- Wait for user approval before proceeding

### Phase 2 — Blender Modeling
- After the spatial strategy is approved, generate a Blender MCP instruction document at `spatial/blender_spec.md`
- This `.md` must contain all information needed to build the 3D model via Blender MCP:
  - Building massing and floor-by-floor geometry
  - Facade design and material assignments
  - Interior layout per floor (walls, openings, key elements)
  - Furniture, display fixtures, and installation placement
  - Lighting setup (architectural light elements, portals, edge glow)
  - Material properties (colors, roughness, transparency, emission)
- The user will provide:
  - **Facade and interior reference images** — use these as the design target
  - **Main camera views / rendering shots** — specific angles that must be set up in Blender
- Use Blender MCP tools (`execute_blender_code`, `get_scene_info`, `get_viewport_screenshot`) to build and verify the model step by step
- Build the model incrementally — floor by floor, verify with viewport screenshots between steps

### Phase 3 — Rendering
- Set up camera positions based on the user-provided main views
- Apply materials, lighting, and environment settings
- Capture viewport screenshots for review
- Iterate based on user feedback
